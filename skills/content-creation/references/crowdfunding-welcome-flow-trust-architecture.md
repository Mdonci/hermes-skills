# Crowdfunding Welcome Flow — Trust Architecture

> Developed from the Lattice Glamping Indiegogo project (May 2026).
> Use with the Welcome Subscriber Flow Pipeline section in content-creation/SKILL.md.

## Why Crowdfunding Welcome Flows Are Different

A crowdfunding waitlist welcome flow is not a purchase nurture. The subscriber is not deciding whether to buy — they are deciding whether to trust a campaign that hasn't launched yet. The central tension:

> "I want this product, but I have been burned by crowdfunding before."

This must be named directly in the flow, not avoided.

## Seven Trust Signals Required

Every crowdfunding welcome flow must address these specific trust dimensions:

| # | Trust Dimension | What to Say | What to Avoid |
|---|----------------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Team | Who they are, where they are, their relevant background. Budapest, Hungary (specific) vs "a team of designers" (vague). | "We are a passionate team of outdoor enthusiasts" |
| 2 | Prototype | Does a working unit exist? Has it been tested? In what conditions? | 3D renders without real photos |
| 3 | Manufacturing | Where it's made, how it's made. "Laser-welded aluminum, EU manufactured" anchors credibility. | "Premium manufacturing partners" (anonymous) |
| 4 | Campaign protection | Honest description of Indiegogo's actual protections, not overclaimed guarantees. | "Indiegogo guarantees your money back" (they don't) |
| 5 | Evidence | Photos and video of the real deployed unit, not just renders. | Renders presented as production units |
| 6 | Timeline | When does production start? When do units ship? Specific, not vague. | "Soon" / "Coming soon" |
| 7 | Why crowdfunding | Honest explanation of why Indiegogo instead of conventional sales (funding first production run, tooling, mold costs). | "Because we believe in community" (fluff) |

## AIM Framework Adaptation for Crowdfunding

### Email 1: Acknowledge (Pathos 70% / Ethos 20% / Logos 10%)
- Mirror the exact pain the product solves (not crowdfunding anxiety — the *product* problem)
- Build emotional resonance around the experience, not the campaign
- One soft trust signal: mention a real prototype exists and has been tested
- No selling, no pricing, no campaign mechanics
- Open loop: origin story (closed in Email 2)

### Email 2: Include (Logos 45% / Ethos 35% / Pathos 20%)
- Lead with proof: manufacturing details, testing, design decisions
- Origin story gives emotional context
- Honest about what crowdfunding is and isn't
- Begin addressing the unspoken trust question
- Open loop: pricing preview (closed in Email 3)

### Email 3: Mobilize (Ethos 35% / Pathos 35% / Logos 30%)
- Synthesis: campaign mechanics, what backing gets you, Indiegogo protection
- Honest risk acknowledgment + concrete de-risking evidence
- Early-bird tier logic, pricing structure, day-one advantage
- Closing vision of the product experience

## Claims to Avoid (Hard Fails)

### Regulatory/Legal
- "Guaranteed to ship by [date]" — unless contractually firm with manufacturer
- "Indiegogo guarantees your money back" — be precise about their actual policy
- "Limited to [X] units" — unless there is a genuine production cap
- "Already in production" — if the campaign funds production
- "Risk-free" — crowdfunding has inherent risk
- "As good as buying retail" — campaign backing supports a project, not a store purchase
- "Prices will never be this low again" — unless documented policy exists

### Product/Trust
- "Revolutionary" / "world's first" / "never before seen"
- Unverifiable competitor comparisons
- Claims beyond tested specifications
- "Military-grade" without sourcing (use the actual credential: "laser-welded aluminum, EU manufactured")
- "Lifetime warranty" unless defined policy exists

## Belief Journey Target

| Stage | Belief |
|-------|--------|
| At subscribe | "This looks interesting. I have no idea if this company is real." |
| After Email 1 | "They understand my pain. This is not generic marketing." |
| After Email 2 | "This is a real product from a real team. I trust them more." |
| After Email 3 | "I know what tier I'm backing and why. The risk feels acceptable. I'm ready for launch day." |

## Post-Sequence Bridge

After the 3-email welcome flow:
- **Campaign not yet live:** Light countdown updates (behind-the-scenes testing, team updates). Keep top of mind without burning engagement.
- **Campaign launch:** Launch notification blast (separate from welcome flow — this is the trigger email).
- **During campaign:** 48-hour and 24-hour early-bird deadline reminders.
- **Post-campaign routing:** Backers → campaign backer update flow. Non-backers → post-campaign retail interest list.

## Example Subject Lines

| Email | Subject | Notes |
|-------|---------|-------|
| Acknowledge | "You have had that camping night too" | Names the shared experience, not the product |
| Include | "Why a Hungarian engineer decided to fix your camping problem" | Specific, intriguing, location-anchors credibility |
| Mobilize | "Here is how the Indiegogo launch works (and what it costs)" | Direct, delivers on promised loop |
