# UGC Management

## Overview

UGC (User-Generated Content) briefs for physical consumer products across multiple markets. This skill covers the methodology for producing creator guides that teach **organic product placement** rather than product-focused advertising content.

## Core Problem We Solve

**The 50% problem:** When creators are given concept briefs with specific scenarios, ~50% will still produce product-first videos (opening on device, listing features, unboxing-style, testimonial-to-camera). This creates content fatigue and ad- immune viewers.

**Root cause:** Structured concepts with shot lists function as **templates** — creators follow them literally instead of developing placement instinct. The more specific the scenario, the more it limits creativity.

### The Anti-Formula Principle

> Teach principles, not templates. Teach instinct, not shot lists.

**Why templates fail:** A template that works for a Budapest apartment fails for a rural Romanian house with grandparents. A Polish family's rhythm ≠ Portuguese family's rhythm. **Every family's authenticity is the only format that matters.**

**What replaces templates:**
- Clear boundary rules (DO's and DON'Ts) — specific, behavioral, testable
- A mental model (the Lens Principle — film around the product, not the product)
- A binary litmus test (the N-Question Test) that catches failures before posting
- Cultural awareness woven into examples, not a separate "diversity" section
- Zero shot lists, zero "Scene 1: Child does X" instructions

## The Organic Placement Methodology

### Core Philosophy
- Viewers have developed ad-immune scroll reflexes. UGC that announces itself as an ad in the first 2 seconds fails.
- The shift: "Here is my life with my child. You'll notice [product] is part of it" — not "Here is [product], let me tell you why it's amazing."
- The product should exist in the frame the way a lamp does — present, real, not the subject.
- The camera finds the family, not the product.

### The Golden Rules Pattern (DO's + DON'Ts)
Every UGC guide must include a thorough DO/DON'T section with:

**DO's (8-10 rules):**
1. Start with the child's face/emotion/environment — not the device
2. Film mid-activity (product already in use), never the setup
3. Show real frustration → exploration → resolution arcs
4. Let the camera linger — context reveals value through duration
5. Film in naturally messy environments (mess = credibility)
6. Use the product as background texture (like a radio)
7. Show parent's hands, not face — never explain to camera
8. End on emotional outcome — product is incidental
9. Film across different times of day and settings
10. Let silence and ambient sound do the work

**DON'Ts (8-10 rules):**
1. Don't open with the product on screen
2. Don't list features — ever (they go in captions)
3. Don't hold the device up to camera (unboxing shot)
4. Don't do face-to-camera testimonials
5. Don't use scripted transitions/zooms/effects on the product
6. Don't narrate benefits — show them happening
7. Don't stage scenes — real toddler chaos performs better
8. Don't make every video the same "breakthrough" arc
9. Don't use tripod or formal camera setups (handheld = authentic)
10. Don't include verbal CTAs in video audio

### The Lens Principle
The core mental model: "I am documenting my life. This product happens to be part of it."

**The filter question:** "Would this shot exist if [product] didn't?" If no → too product-focused. If yes → organic.

**The lamp analogy:** A lamp illuminates a scene without being its subject. The product should appear the same way.

**The 80/20 rule:** 80% real life, 20% product presence. Product never focal point for more than 5 consecutive seconds.

### The N-Question Test (Litmus Gate)
Every video must pass before posting. Add more questions per product/tier:

1. **Q1 (Structural):** Does the video still work if [product] is removed? (Yes = good)
2. **Q2 (Opening):** What does the first frame show? (Product = FAIL)
3. **Q3 (Emotional):** Would viewer say "aww cute kid" before "what's that device"? (Yes = success)
4. **Q4 (Audio):** Does the audio contain any sales language? (Yes = FAIL)

### Market-Specific Integration
Cultural texture must be **woven through the guide**, not relegated to a token section:
- Multi-generational households (grandparent involvement)
- Outdoor/market/countryside summer rhythms
- Specific visual cues (slippers, rugs, lived-in kitchens)
- Bilingual context relevance
- Native-language grandparent terms (*nagymama*, *babcia*, *bunica*, *babička*, *stará mama*, *avó*)
- Different parenting expression across cultures (reserved vs. expressive)

### CTA Rule
- CTAs live in captions only — never in video audio
- Caption tone: parent-to-parent recommendation, not brand CTA
- Product features, guarantee, shipping, ebook → caption only
- "Link in bio" → simple, unforced, in caption
- Captions in native language, not English (unless audience demands it)
- One narrow exception: child-directed natural mention in passing ("give me the cards")
- When in doubt, keep it silent

## Multi-Market UGC Programs

### When launching across 8+ markets:
- The guide must work universally, not feel Western/Northern Euro-centric
- Explicitly name all markets in the guide intro
- Use native-language terms from each market
- Give each market at least one specific example
- Address uneven cultural texture distribution (market with less coverage gets specific attention)
- The "Anti-Formula" principle means creators adapt the guide to their culture — not the other way around

### Creator types to include:
- Working parents (morning/evening routines)
- Multi-generational households (grandparent involvement)
- Summer exodus families (countryside, cottage, village)
- Bilingual/multilingual families (relevant to product features)
